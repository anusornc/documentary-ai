# A Brief History of Calculus — Script v1

## Thesis
Calculus grew from ancient geometric approximations to a dual discovery by Newton and Leibniz that triggered a scientific war, before being formalized into the ultimate language of change and modern intelligence.

## Beats

### EP1: เรขาคณิตของการเปลี่ยนแปลง (The Geometry of Change)
- **Hook**: How do you measure the speed of a falling object at one exact instant?
- **Archimedes' Exhaustion**: Ancient Greeks could calculate static shapes, but curves were impossible. Archimedes used infinite polygons to "exhaust" the area of a circle.
- **The Speed Dilemma**: Standard geometry fails for moving things. Speed is distance divided by time. But at an "instant," time is zero. You cannot divide by zero!
- **Newton's Isolation**: During the Great Plague of 1665, a young Isaac Newton fled to the countryside and created the "method of fluxions" to link motion and curves.
- **Outro**: Newton solved the math of motion, but kept his work secret. That silence would spark the most famous war in math history.

### EP2: สงครามนิยามและสัญลักษณ์ (The Infinitesimal War)
- **Hook**: Can two geniuses discover the exact same mathematical universe, yet turn it into a battlefield of plagiarism?
- **Leibniz's Insight**: In the 1670s, Gottfried Leibniz independently created calculus, focusing on beautiful, intuitive notations like $dy/dx$ and $\int$.
- **The Controversy**: Newton's followers accused Leibniz of stealing Newton's secret work. A nationalistic battle split the British Royal Society and Europe.
- **Notation Victory**: Though Newton won the political battle, Leibniz's notation won the future. It was simply too clean and powerful.
- **Outro**: The war over who invented it was over, but a greater crisis remained: the logic itself was built on "infinitesimal" values that shouldn't exist.

### EP3: กติกาใหม่และการคำนวณอนาคต (The Bedrock of Change)
- **Hook**: How do you build a logical foundation for values that are "almost zero, but not quite"?
- **The Infinitesimal Crisis**: Early calculus divided by $dx$. Critics laughed, calling these "ghosts of departed quantities."
- **Cauchy & Weierstrass**: In the 1800s, mathematicians rebuilt calculus using the epsilon-delta limit definition. Infinitesimals were replaced by rigorous bounds.
- **Modern Gradients**: Today, calculus doesn't just calculate planetary orbits. It calculates the gradients in machine learning that train ChatGPT.
- **Outro**: What started as Archimedes' polygons is now the mathematical engine of human technology and artificial minds.
