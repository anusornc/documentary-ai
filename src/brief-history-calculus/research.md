## Thesis
Calculus emerged from ancient geometric approximations (Archimedes) into an independent double-discovery (Newton and Leibniz) that sparked a bitter controversy, before being logically anchored by the concept of limits to run modern physics and artificial intelligence.

## Key facts (with sources)
- **Archimedes and Exhaustion**: In ancient Greece, Archimedes used the method of exhaustion to find areas under curves by inscribing infinite polygons, foreshadowing integration — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion
- **Newton's Fluxions (1665)**: During the Great Plague of London, Newton isolated himself and developed his "method of fluxions" to calculate planetary motions and instantaneous changes — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton
- **Leibniz's Notation (1675)**: Gottfried Leibniz independently developed calculus, introducing the integral sign ($\int$) and differential notation ($d$), which was much easier to use than Newton's notation — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz
- **The Priority Dispute**: In the early 1700s, a bitter controversy erupted over who invented calculus first. The Royal Society (led by Newton) accused Leibniz of plagiarism, creating a nationalistic split in mathematics — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz%E2%80%93Newton_calculus_controversy
- **Ghosts of Departed Quantities**: Philosopher George Berkeley famously criticized the logical foundation of calculus and infinitesimals in 1734, calling them "the ghosts of departed quantities" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Analyst
- **Cauchy & Weierstrass Limits**: In the 19th century, Augustin-Louis Cauchy and Karl Weierstrass formalized limits using the epsilon-delta ($\epsilon$-$\delta$) formulation, anchoring calculus in rigorous logic without infinitesimals — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(%CE%B5,_%CE%B4)-definition_of_limit
